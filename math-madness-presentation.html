The Problem

Students were
disengaging
from review.

Instructional design challenge

Review worksheets went consistently incomplete — students lacked motivation to engage with passive study materials before the final exam.

The final exam covered 8 major domains from the full school year, requiring students to synthesize and retrieve content across all units simultaneously.

Traditional study guides and practice problems failed to produce the engagement or retention needed for comprehensive cumulative review.

A new instructional approach was needed — one that would make students want to prepare.

Design Approach

Structured as a 7-Day March Madness Tournament

01
Round Robin Seeding

All 200 students competed across multiple rounds to earn bracket placement based on accumulated points — ensuring broad participation before elimination play.

02
16-Team Bracket

Seeded teams competed head-to-head in daily elimination rounds, mirroring the March Madness format students already knew and cared about.

03
Tiered Problem Sets

Problems worth 2 or 3 points based on cognitive demand — a direct application of Bloom's Taxonomy. Every learner had an entry point; higher complexity was rewarded.

04
Data Tracking & Debrief

Google Sheets dashboard tracked scores in real time, surfacing class-wide learning gaps for targeted reteaching between rounds before the final exam.

Outcomes & Results

The tournament moved the needle.

Exam Performance

Final exam scores increased by 12% following implementation. Students also showed improvement on PSSA assessment results, outperforming prior cohorts who used traditional review methods.

Student Engagement

Dramatic increase in voluntary engagement with review content. Tournament problem completion rates significantly exceeded traditional worksheet completion rates.

Real-Time Data Use

Live Google Sheets dashboard surfaced class-wide knowledge gaps between rounds, enabling targeted reteaching of specific domains before the final exam.

Learner Motivation

The competitive bracket format created intrinsic motivation — students voluntarily studied outside class to prepare for daily rounds, behavior rarely seen during traditional review.

Designer Reflection

"

The core design challenge was getting students to voluntarily engage with content they had been avoiding for weeks. By reframing review as competition — with real stakes, public standings, and a format they recognized — the same material that felt like a chore became something students prepared for.

The tiered point system (2-point vs. 3-point shots) was intentional: it gave every learner an entry point while rewarding students willing to attempt higher-complexity problems — a direct application of differentiation and Bloom's Taxonomy in a competitive context.

If I were to iterate on this design, I would build in a structured reflection component after each round — converting competitive energy into metacognitive awareness and helping students self-identify which domains needed the most additional review before exam day.
